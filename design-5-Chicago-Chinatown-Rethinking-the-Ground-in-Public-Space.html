Chicago Chinatown
Rethinking the Ground in Public Space

Location: Chinatown, Chicago, IL
Time: Spring semester, 2020
Course: Surface Design Studio - Depave Chicago
Affilitation: Landscape Architecture Department, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign.
Category: Surface / Design / Soil /Landscape Urbanism

Satellite picture of Chicago Chinatown - full of grey infrastructure

Physical, material ground conditions play a key role in the ecological, social, and aesthetic life of cities. The ground mediates human perception of one’s environment, determines rain’s movements and patterns, and regulates the air temperatures of a city both locally and across the cityscape.

In Chicago’s Chinatown upwards of 90% of the ground surface is paved, with public space comprised of asphalt streets or plazas with pavers crumbling through differential settlement. This monolith of pavement that lazily served the economic market-place through a set of outdated 20th century urban standards can be transformed to reflect a contemporary attention to material alternatives and the cultural life of Chinatown. This project proposes pavement alternatives that greatly increase rainwater absorption, lower urban heat island, and enhance the vibrant food and shopping culture of Chinatown.
