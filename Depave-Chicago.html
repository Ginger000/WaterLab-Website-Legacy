Depave Chicago

Depave Chicago is an ongoing design research project aimed at transforming the ground surfaces of post-industrial, infrastructure-intense cities into more ecologically and socially just environments. If you want to understand your city better, look at the ground - is it designed for you the pedestrian or a car? Is it designed to embrace water or to get rid of it and send it downstream?

In 2015, Depave Chicago started as a series of design studios about urban flooding, pavement, public space design, and green infrastructure - accompanied by geological studies to correlate surface and subsurface soils conditions to identify a series of site locations to alleviate urban flooding. More recent research has focused on urban surface design to promote infiltration of rainwater into the geologic coastal substrata of Chicago. The target soil areas are the locations of the former dune ridges of the Chicago lakeshore that were paved over during urbanization. Depave Chicago proposes to tap into those soils as an ecological infrastructure for the city and to celebrate Chicago's natural heritage through that design work which is currently invisible to the people of Chicago.

Water Lab has since been studying imperviousness and ground plane material conditions more broadly, relative to climate change and design equity, as many of the urban flooding and urban heat island issues in low-wealth and BIPOC communities can be contributed to urban ground conditions. We are investigating concentrations of imperviousness over 25% and 60% relative to combined sewer areas and areas of environmental injustice. Water Lab is also coordinating its efforts with the Chicago River Watershed Council to identify public lands projects, conservation areas, and disruptive design practices to replace asphalt with vegetation and green/blue infrastructure throughout neighborhoods.
