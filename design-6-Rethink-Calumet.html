Regional and site analysis of Calumet

A legacy of steel production and ongoing industrial production has left the Chicago-Calumet region contaminated and flood prone, imposing stress and health concerns on communities and residents. The network of industrial sites that sits among wetlands and borders the Calumet River, although sources of economic growth, also serve as barriers to residents and wildlife. Not only have the people of Calumet been cut off from the land and water, but their connections to the history of the land and the people who historically occupied this land there have been buried under years of industrialization.

Rethinking Calumet investigates the conditions that have stagnated the region and propose innovative strategies to establish environmental and social systems that work for the people who live there. A pivotal aspect of this is calling into question inefficient land uses - especially those on industrial sites - and encouraging multi-purpose functionality that can solve issues facing the region.

Site examples

Stony Island Avenue is one of the more egregious examples of this, in which the entire site is covered with impermeable pavement and utilized as a massive parking lot. Its only function is to hold cars with little thought to stormwater runoff or heat island effects. Most of this parking could be condensed into vertical car storage while the rest of the site is restored to a wetland environment. This wetland restoration would join Big Marsh and Indian Ridge to its north, creating a web of linked ecosystems which can provide more runoff relief and recreational opportunities as a network than alone. These restored wetlands lead into a trail that follows the historic footprint of Lake Calumet, serving as an educational tool to remind and connect people to the history of the land. Lake Calumet has seen drastic changes to its edges as industries manipulated the land for its benefit, leaving it unrecognizable to its former bounds and inaccessible for most people. Marking historic edges, restoring the land, and educating people about the two brings Calumet's past closer to its present, allowing residents to consider what the land once was - and more importantly, what it could be.


Civic engagement

One of the most valuable opportunities for reconnecting residents to the land is through civic engagement. Through hands-on opportunities like planting community rain gardens or cleaning up creeks, people can begin to reflect on the water system in their area. Encouraging water mindfulness through public workshops on rain barrels, permeable paving, and native plantings, they can learn what their personal options are for contributing to a water-smart future. Even children will be able to participate, learning about nature and water through school and library programs. Through the construction of rain gardens, creek clean ups, and encourage water mindfulness with rain barrels, permeable pavement, and plantings. Involving citizens through civic engagement and education is a way of reclaiming land for the public sphere which has been previously controlled by industries; this reconnects the community to the natural history of the land and also encourages further investment in that land.


Phased transformation

Rethinking Calumet will occur in phases, all built upon integrating communities with new ideas and transformations while engaging their residents in the process. This project is set up in phases that help integrate the newly proposed ideas and transformations with the surrounding communities while also engaging them in the process. Community engagement is imperative to reclaiming the land of Calumet for its residents. 32% of the region is comprised of residential neighborhoods, presenting a massive opportunity to reach people at the community level. Through schools and libraries, the construction of rain gardens, installation of rain barrels, and green roofs all act as tools not only to slow down stormwater and reduce runoff, but to educate and break a large-scale problem into more manageable pieces. Demonstrations and workshops hosted at community centers increase transparency of the interventions taking place across the region and allow for community input. Increased frequency and transparency in toxicity testing can help guide community focus as they reclaim the land.
