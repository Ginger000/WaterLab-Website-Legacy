A Community Plan for Integrated Water Design
For this project and community, we researched, modeled, and designed a plan to adapt to the heaviest rainstorms of the region.

This 250-acre neighborhood design is part of a research project that investigates how to retrofit cities and neighborhoods through green infrastructure to address urban flooding. The research explores regional geological history and how the native sub-soils that have been paved-over are an important part of better stormwater design across the region. Rarely is the pre-urban geological landscape and its extant soils considered a design “input” in green stormwater infrastructure planning in the region. Through an investigation of native sub-soils--and their range of infiltration potentials--we created a series of design interventions to absorb, hold, sponge, and filter the largest rain events--through the land itself.

Working with the Village of Midlothian, a tireless community who are extremely motivated to solve recurring urban flooding, the team of landscape architects, geologists, and civil and environmental engineers developed a neighborhood-scale rainwater design project as an example of urban wet-resilience.
