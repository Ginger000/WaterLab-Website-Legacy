Soils Fieldwork

A major misunderstanding of the Chicago-Calumet region has to do with the soils under the city.

The soils of the region have been wholesale lumped into the category of “blue clay” - the infamous moniker of a hard impervious, impenetrable soil composed of glacial till (mixed texture soil) on which the City is constructed. But, this is not the case.

Our team, guided by Quaternary geologists has been ‘unearthing’ historic soils maps, soil borings, and water well and engineering reports to assemble a more accurate story of the geologic history of the region, and to characterize the soils here today. The land area inside the terminal moraine (the topographic edge of the Great Lakes basin) has a 14,000 year land development history—a story of glacial retreat, glacial lake plains, lake bottoms, and coastal formation—that is all composed of a wide diversity of soil types and textures from clayey loam in the lake bottom depressional areas to coarse sands and gravels in the former beach ridges and dune-swale locations. The patterns of these soils reveals a fascinating glaciated landscape history.

There have been two significant phases of soils work to date:

In 2016: McGuire and her research assistants Alan Cong and David Zhang spent time in the Illinois Geological Records Unit, pouring through engineering logs of the soils in Chicago using GIS and Rhino to create new maps and cross-sections of the soils in the upper 50’ of the city. Funding support was provided by Wright-Ingraham Institute and a matching grant from the Illinois’ College of Fine & Applied Arts.

Soil Sample Diagram 2

In 2018: Led by Drew Phillips and David Grimley, our team conducted soil borings, measuring saturated hydraulic conductivity, and remapped the Calumet region. We then used this data in creating stormwater designs, specific to the variety of underlying soils in the region. Funding support by NOAA Illinois-Indiana Sea Grant.

During this period, students learned from Quaternary geologists how to take a soil core, and properly record changes in the soil along the profile. Soil scientists from the Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS/USDA) trained our team to operate the Amoozameters. Amoozameters measure Ksat (saturated hydraulic conductivity - the measure of how quickly (or not) water infiltrates through saturated soils). Typically in a field test, we set out 6-9 of these, in a grid, set to varying depths of soil. At least three Amoozameters are assigned at each depth, to provide an average across the units.These instruments are somewhat unique in this type of research. The instruments were loaned to us for three months and for free! to assist us in our work. Soil cores were characterized on site and also taken into the soils lab at University of Illinois for moisture content and grain size analysis.
